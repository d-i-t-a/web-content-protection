Chapter 1: The Highway

The email arrived at 3:47 AM, because catastrophe has no respect for time zones.

Aferdita read it in bed, one eye squinted against the blue glow: "Your entire fall catalog is on a pirate site. All 240 titles. Already seeded to twelve mirror sites."

She didn't scream. She didn't swear. She lay very still and watched the ceiling fan make its slow, stupid circles.

She called Dion. Her head of engineering answered on the first ring, which meant he already knew.

"The web reader," he said. "I told the board. You put a book in a browser, and the browser is the user's machine."

"We don't need a vault," he said finally. "We need a very good fence."

Chapter 2: The Menu

Monday morning. Whiteboard. Three coffees on the table, all black, all already half-empty.

Dion's solution was elegant. Every copy event, every cut event, every keyboard shortcut that could possibly invoke them — intercepted at the door.

"You don't have to stop every thief," Dion said. "You just have to make stealing feel like stealing."

Chapter 3: Catch and Release

The drag vulnerability was Aria's favorite party trick. She would select two paragraphs of text, drag them to a text editor.

Dion closed the vulnerability in an afternoon. Every dragstart event suppressed.

Chapter 4: The Cipher

Text obfuscation was the team's most ambitious lock. All text outside the reader's visible viewport would be scrambled in the DOM itself.

"What if someone saves the page?" Aria asked. "They get gibberish," Dion said.

Chapter 5: Zero Width

Watermarking was invisible and inescapable. Every copy carried a fingerprint back to the user.

Chapter 6: The Last Line

Image protection was the final module. Pointer-events set to none prevented right-click menus.

"A determined attacker will always find a way," Dion said. "But a casual thief? We've made it so inconvenient that buying the book is easier than stealing it."

Nineteen modules. Nineteen locks. "Ship it," she said.
