Part I

The Breach

The email arrived at 3:47 AM, because catastrophe has no respect for time zones.

Aferdita read it in bed, one eye squinted against the blue glow: "Your entire fall catalog is on a pirate site. All 240 titles. Already seeded to twelve mirror sites."

She didn't scream. She didn't swear. She lay very still and watched the ceiling fan make its slow, stupid circles. Two years of her authors' work. Fourteen months of her team's engineering. Gone — or rather, everywhere, which was worse than gone.

She called Dion. Her head of engineering answered on the first ring, which meant he already knew.

"The web reader," he said. No greeting, no preamble. "I told the board. You put a book in a browser, and the browser is the user's machine. It's their DOM. Their JavaScript console. Their clipboard. We handed them the keys and asked them nicely not to make copies."

Aferdita stared at the ceiling fan. "Dedicated apps?"

"Our readers would riot. Nobody downloads apps anymore to read books."

"Then what?"

A pause. She heard Dion's espresso machine gurgling — the man was already in his kitchen, already thinking. "We don't need a vault," he said finally. "We need a very good fence."

Aferdita smiled despite herself. "How many locks?"

"As many as it takes."

* * *

Chapter 1: The Highway

Monday morning. Whiteboard. Three coffees on the table, all black, all already half-empty. Dion stood at the board with a red marker.

"The clipboard," he said, drawing a thick arrow from a box labeled BOOK to a box labeled WORLD, "is a six-lane highway out of your application. Right now we have no toll booths, no speed cameras, and the signs say 'Free Parking.'"

Aria, the youngest engineer on the team and already the most dangerous, demonstrated: she selected a full page of text, pressed Ctrl+C, opened her email, and pressed Ctrl+V. The entire chapter appeared, perfectly formatted, in a draft to herself.

"Thirty seconds," she said. "I just stole chapter four."

Dion's solution was elegant. Every copy event, every cut event, every keyboard shortcut that could possibly invoke them — intercepted at the door. In block mode, the clipboard received nothing but a polite placeholder. In restrict mode, readers could copy a sentence, maybe two — enough to tweet a quote, never enough to reconstruct a page.

It was Aferdita who suggested the touch that made it beautiful: attribution. Any copied text arrived trailing a line — "From 'Nineteen Locks' — (c) DITA Publishing." The words carried their birth certificate with them.

"You don't have to stop every thief," Dion said. "You just have to make stealing feel like stealing."

Aria frowned. "What if they need to copy for a review? A citation?"

"Then we let them." Dion drew a switch on the board. "Citation mode. The host app flips a flag, the user copies what they need, the flag flips back. Generosity with a leash."

Chapter 2: The Menu

Tuesday. Aria arrived early, set down her laptop, and without a word right-clicked on the reader's homepage. The context menu appeared: Save As... View Page Source... Inspect...

Dion watched from across the room. "You're making a point."

"I'm making your point." She right-clicked an image. Save Image As... She right-clicked a footnote link. Copy Link Address. "The context menu is a vending machine for content extraction. You just right-click and choose what you want."

Dion groaned. "It's so obvious I'm embarrassed we didn't—"

"Fix it on day one? Yeah." Aria grinned. "The good news is it's a one-liner. Intercept contextmenu on every element we care about. preventDefault(). Done."

"The users who right-click to look up a word—"

"Will survive," Aria said. "And the users who right-click to steal a book? Won't."

Chapter 3: Catch and Release

The drag vulnerability was Aria's favorite party trick. She would select two paragraphs of text, hold down the mouse button, and — in one smooth motion — drag the selection out of the browser window and drop it directly into a text editor on her desktop.

The first time she demonstrated it to the team, the room went silent.

"No copy event fires," she said, gesturing at the console. "No clipboard involved. The OS handles the transfer natively. It's a secret tunnel under the building."

Dion stared at the stolen text sitting in Notepad, pristine and complete. "That's... violating."

"That's dragstart," Aria corrected. "And we kill it." She snapped her fingers. "Every dragstart event on protected elements — intercepted, prevented. Text, images, links. Nothing leaves the reader. Try to drag it, the cursor just sits there looking disappointed."

She paused. "It's like bolting the paintings to the gallery wall. You can admire them. You just can't tuck one under your coat."

Chapter 4: Gutenberg's Ghost

The call from London came on a drizzly Wednesday. A bookshop in Shoreditch — the kind with exposed brick and a curated selection of "interesting" nonfiction — had been caught selling photocopied versions of DITA's bestselling cookbook. Beautiful, full-color, spiral-bound photocopies. Someone had Ctrl+P'd every page to PDF and walked it to a copy shop.

"Print," Dion said, pinching the bridge of his nose. "The oldest attack in the history of publishing. Gutenberg would laugh."

"Gutenberg would cry," Aferdita corrected. "He spent his life's savings on that printing press and died broke."

The print protection module came together in an afternoon. Three layers, because Dion didn't believe in single points of failure. First: a CSS @media print rule that made every protected element invisible — the print preview saw only blank paper. Second: the beforeprint and afterprint events, blanking content the instant the print dialog opened and restoring it the instant it closed. Third: keyboard interception — Ctrl+P caught and swallowed before the browser ever got involved.

"Belt, suspenders, and a really tight belt," Aria summarized.

"If the first two fail," Dion said, "the third one catches it. If you manage to somehow bypass all three..." He shrugged. "At that point you've earned it."

Chapter 5: Thirteen Doors

Aria bought a whiteboard marker and wrote in capital letters across the top: ESCAPE ROUTES

Below it, she listed every keyboard shortcut a savvy user might try:

Ctrl+S — Save the page
Ctrl+U — View source
Ctrl+Shift+I — DevTools
F12 — DevTools (again)
Ctrl+Shift+J — Console
Ctrl+Shift+C — Element picker
Ctrl+A — Select all
Ctrl+P — Print
...and five more on Mac

"Thirteen shortcuts," she said, capping the marker. "Thirteen doors out of the building. We lock every single one."

The keyboard module listened for keydown everywhere — the document, the iframes, the window itself. Each shortcut was matched and silently consumed. The browser never saw the keypress. The user pressed F12 and... nothing happened. Ctrl+S and... nothing. It was like shouting into a pillow.

"We're borrowing their keys," Aria said cheerfully. "They can have them back when they leave the reader."

Dion added one more thing: a configurable passthrough list. Because some publishers wanted Ctrl+F for search, and some wanted Ctrl+C in specific panels. "A good lock has a good key," he said. "You just have to choose who gets one."

Chapter 6: The Cipher

Friday afternoon. Dion had been quiet all day — never a good sign. He called Aferdita into his office, turned his monitor so she could see, and said: "Open your browser's developer console."

She typed document.body.innerText and pressed Enter.

The entire book spilled out. Every word, every chapter, every secret — pasted neatly into the console. No copy protection triggered. No drag prevention engaged. Just... the whole text, sitting there, asking to be stolen.

"The DOM is a public library," Dion said. "Anyone with a developer console has a library card."

"So we close the library?"

"No." Dion smiled — the smile he reserved for ideas he was proud of. "We rewrite the books. In real time. While the reader is reading."

He explained: imagine a physical book where every page you aren't looking at is written in nonsense. The moment you turn to it, the words rearrange into English. The moment you turn away, they scramble again. You can read the page in front of you. But you can never photograph the whole book at once, because most of it doesn't exist in readable form.

"Text obfuscation," Dion said. "We track the scroll position. Text inside the viewport — readable. Text outside the viewport — scrambled. Characters shuffled within each word. It looks like language. It's gibberish."

Aferdita tried document.body.innerText again. This time, the output was a wall of nonsense punctuated by the few sentences currently visible on screen.

"That's..." she searched for the word. "That's mean."

"That's effective," Dion corrected.

Part II

The Arms Race

Chapter 7: The Gap

They'd locked thirteen keyboard shortcuts. But DevTools could still be opened from the browser's hamburger menu, from a right-click, from an extension, or — Aria's favorite — by launching Chrome with a command-line flag.

"We can't prevent DevTools from existing," Dion said on Monday. "It's the browser's own feature. We can't fight the browser."

"We don't need to fight it," Aria replied. She had her laptop open to a blank page. She docked DevTools to the right side of the window. "Look at the page width. What changed?"

Dion leaned in. window.outerWidth stayed the same. window.innerWidth had shrunk by 400 pixels.

"The gap," Aria said. "When you dock DevTools, the inner width drops by the width of the panel. No toolbar, no bookmarks bar, no sidebar — nothing else creates a gap that large. Two hundred pixels is our tripwire."

The DevTools detection module polled the delta every second. When it exceeded the threshold — 200 pixels horizontally or vertically — the content disappeared. Storage cleared. Console wiped. Or, for gentler publishers, just a callback: someone is looking under the hood.

"And for the really paranoid?" Dion asked.

"Optional dependency: devtools-detector. Catches undocked panels too."

Aferdita framed the policy for the board: "When someone opens DevTools, they're not reading a book. They're taking it apart. So the book politely excuses itself."

Chapter 8: The Three-Second Reader

The pattern in the server logs was almost beautiful in its precision. One account. One IP. Loading page after page after page — every three seconds, like a metronome. It had consumed twenty-six complete books in a single afternoon.

"No human reads that fast," Dion said. "Not even Aria, and she reads in the shower."

"I do not read in the—" Aria started.

"Puppeteer," Dion continued. "Or Playwright. Or Selenium. A headless browser, scripted to turn pages and dump text. Our entire catalog, scraped in an afternoon."

Aria cracked her knuckles. This was her specialty. She built the detection cascade: five checks, layered like a fingerprint.

First: navigator.webdriver. Automation frameworks set this flag and couldn't hide it. Second: the user agent string — "HeadlessChrome" was a dead giveaway. Third: browser plugins — real browsers shipped with plugins; headless instances had none. Fourth: global variables like __selenium_unwrapped, __webdriver_evaluate, _phantom — each framework left its own fingerprints. Fifth: screen dimensions — headless browsers often reported impossible resolutions.

"Any one of these could be a false positive," Aria said. "Two together? Suspicious. Three or more? That's a robot."

And the killer feature: periodic re-checking. Because a clever attacker might load the page normally and inject automation afterward. "Every five seconds," Aria said, "we look again. The disguise has to hold up all day."

Chapter 9: White Out

Someone posted a perfect screenshot of Chapter 12 on Twitter. Full resolution, DITA's proprietary serif font, even the annotation highlights visible in the sidebar. Four thousand retweets by lunch. The author was livid. The publisher was livider.

"We can't prevent screenshots," Dion said before anyone asked. "The operating system handles PrintScreen, not the browser. We don't control the OS."

"So what can we do?"

"We can make the content disappear the instant they try."

The screenshot detection module was beautifully paranoid. It watched for visibilitychange — tab switched? Content gone. It watched for blur on the window — focus moved to Snipping Tool, ShareX, any overlay app? Content gone. It listened for the PrintScreen key on keyup.

When triggered, every protected element flipped to visibility: hidden in the same frame. The screenshot captured a blank page. A fraction of a second later, when the user returned, the content faded back in as if nothing had happened.

"It's a magician's trick," Aria said. "The card is only in the hat while someone is watching."

"And the ten percent who use a camera pointed at the screen?" Aferdita asked.

"That's what watermarking is for," Dion said. "Chapter ten."

Chapter 10: Zero Width

Dion gathered the team in the conference room, dimmed the lights, and projected a page of text onto the wall. "What do you see?"

"Chapter three," Aria said.

"Look closer."

"...Chapter three?"

Dion adjusted the projector's contrast to maximum. Faintly, diagonally, repeating across the entire page in barely-visible text: user-42 • 2026-03-20T14:32:00Z • user-42 • 2026-03-20T14:32:00Z

"Three percent opacity," Dion said. "Invisible during normal reading. But if this screenshot appears on a pirate site, you adjust the levels in Photoshop and..." He spread his hands. "Hello, User 42."

But the visual watermark was only layer one. Layer two was stranger. Dion pasted a block of text into a hex editor and pointed at characters that rendered as absolutely nothing — no width, no height, no visible representation whatsoever.

"Zero-width Unicode characters," he said. "U+200B, U+200C, U+200D, U+FEFF. Invisible. But present. We encode the user's ID in binary using combinations of these characters and inject them throughout the text."

Aferdita stared. "So if someone copies the text and pastes it anywhere..."

"The fingerprint travels with it. Invisible. Indestructible. Survives copy, paste, email, even OCR in some cases. And we can decode the ID from any leaked text with one function call."

"That's not a watermark," Aria said. "That's a DNA signature."

Chapter 11: The Tape Measure

The call from Oxford University Press was diplomatically worded but unmistakable: Your protection is too aggressive. Our readers are academics. They need to select text to cite it. Fix this or we're switching providers.

Aferdita asked the obvious question: "How much text does an academic need to select at once?"

The answer, after three meetings, two focus groups, and one surprisingly heated debate about the average length of a proper citation: about two sentences. Maybe three for a block quote. Never a full page. Never a full paragraph, even.

"So let them select," Dion said. "Just... not too much."

The selection limiting module watched selectionchange events. As a user's selection grew, it measured the character count in real time. At 200 characters — or 300, or whatever the publisher configured — the selection stopped expanding. The user could highlight a sentence, copy a quote, annotate a passage. But triple-click to select a paragraph? Ctrl+A to select the page? The selection collapsed back to the threshold.

"It's a tape measure," Aria said. "Extends to three feet. Perfectly useful for hanging a picture. Useless for surveying a football field."

Oxford was delighted. Their researchers could cite. Their books couldn't be bulk-selected. Everyone went home happy.

Chapter 12: The Berlin Paper

The vulnerability report arrived from a graduate student at Humboldt University in Berlin, and it was the most creative attack any of them had ever seen.

The student had written a script that loaded each page of an ebook, extracted the visible text, fed it into speechSynthesis.speak(), recorded the browser's audio output, and piped the recording through Whisper — an open-source speech-to-text model — to reconstruct the original text with 97% accuracy.

Text to speech. Speech to audio. Audio back to text. A Rube Goldberg machine for book theft.

"Who even thinks of this?" Aria said, laughing despite herself.

"A person with a PhD and no publishing contract," Dion said drily. "Block it."

The speech synthesis module patched speechSynthesis.speak() at the prototype level. In block mode, any attempt to speak text simply... didn't. The method returned normally, no error thrown, no audio produced. In restrict mode, utterances were truncated to a configurable character limit — enough for an accessibility tooltip, not enough to reconstruct a page.

"What about real screen readers?" Aferdita asked immediately.

"Different API entirely," Aria said. "Screen readers use the Accessibility Object Model — ARIA attributes, the accessibility tree. speechSynthesis is the Web Speech API. Completely separate. Blocking one doesn't touch the other."

The Berlin student, when informed, wrote back a gracious note: Fair enough. I'll find another thesis topic.

Chapter 13: Sand Timer

The VP of Marketing arrived at the engineering standup uninvited — always a sign of impending trouble.

"Our free preview program," she began, "is being exploited."

The numbers were damning. Twelve percent of preview users kept their browser tabs open indefinitely, slowly reading the entire book over days or weeks without ever purchasing. One user had somehow kept a preview tab alive for forty-three days.

"Forty-three days?" Dion said. "That's not a preview. That's a residency."

The content expiration module was simple and ruthless. A countdown started when the session began — configurable by the publisher. One hour for a preview. A full day for a rental. As the timer wound down, a warning fired: fifteen minutes remaining, five minutes, one minute. When time expired, the content blanked. Vanished. Like writing in sand at high tide.

For paying readers, the session could be extended — a button, a click, another thirty minutes. Up to a maximum. For preview readers, extensions were limited: three chances, then the book was truly gone.

"It's a library book," Aferdita told the marketing team. "It has a due date. And unlike a library, we don't charge late fees. We just close the cover."

Part III

The Last Line

Chapter 14: The Frame

The children's book division was bleeding. Not words — pictures. Their illustrators' artwork, six months of watercolor and digital painting per book, was appearing on Pinterest boards, Etsy prints, and phone cases within hours of publication.

Fig 1. — The DITA offices at dusk (try right-clicking or dragging this image)

"Five vectors," Aria said, holding up her hand. "Right-click save. Drag to desktop. Long-press on mobile. Open image in new tab. Read the src from the DOM." She folded each finger down as she listed them. "Five ways to steal a picture, and the browser helps with every single one."

The image protection module fought on every front. Pointer events disabled — no right-click context menu on images. Transparent overlays positioned above each image — "Save Image As" captured a blank pixel. Drag events intercepted on every <img>. For the nuclear option: images rendered onto <canvas> elements, the originals replaced entirely. Canvas can't be right-click-saved. And for one final trick: source URLs converted to blob: URLs, so even inspecting the DOM revealed nothing useful.

"It's a painting in a museum," Aria said. "Glass in front, bolted to the wall, no photography allowed. You can stand there and admire it all day. You just can't take it home."

Chapter 15: Breadcrumbs

Aferdita noticed it on a Tuesday afternoon. She was hovering over a footnote in the reader, and the browser's status bar helpfully displayed: https://cdn.dita-publishing.com/api/v2/content/9781234567890/media/supplementary-figure-4.pdf?token=eyJhbGciO...

She stared at it for a long time.

"Dion," she said, very calmly, "our CDN paths, API structure, and authentication tokens are visible in the status bar of every link in every book."

Hover over these links with Link URL Hiding enabled and check your status bar: EPUB 3.3 Specification · Readium Architecture · W3C Publishing

The fix was almost comically simple: strip the href attribute, store the real URL in a data-href, navigate programmatically on click. The link still looked like a link — underlined, colored, clickable. But the status bar showed nothing. The URL was invisible until the moment of navigation.

"We're not hiding the destination," Dion clarified. "We're hiding the breadcrumbs. The user still gets where they're going. They just can't see the path until they're on it."

Chapter 16: The Bouncer

The forensic report on the piracy breach contained a footnote that changed everything: 70% of automated extractions originated from a single browser — a Chromium fork that ships with developer tools permanently enabled and content protection APIs deliberately disabled.

Aria read it twice. Then she laughed — the bitter kind. "They built a browser specifically for stealing content."

"And we're supposed to serve them books in it," Dion said.

"Not anymore."

The browser enforcement module was a bouncer: a whitelist of approved browsers — Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge, Opera — checked against navigator.userAgent on load. Unrecognized browsers got the door. No content loaded. No error to inspect. Just a polite message: This reader is not supported in your browser.

"What about niche but legitimate browsers?" Aferdita asked.

"Configurable whitelist," Aria said. "And an optional dependency on browserslist-useragent-regexp for publishers who want version-specific rules. 'Chrome 90 and above,' that kind of thing."

Dion summed it up: "You want to read our books? Use a real browser. It's not a high bar."

Chapter 17: The Needle Drop

The educational division had a problem that the fiction division didn't: audio and video. EPUB3 made it possible to embed media directly in ebooks — pronunciation guides, lecture clips, experiment demonstrations. Beautiful in theory. A piracy nightmare in practice.

Audiobook Narration — Chapter 17

With Media Protection enabled: try right-clicking, downloading, or inspecting the source URL

"Right-click, Save Audio As," Aria demonstrated for the hundredth time. "Or just open DevTools, find the network tab, and download the original file. The src attribute is right there in the HTML."

The media protection module was thorough to the point of obsession. The native download button? CSS-hidden. The controlslist="nodownload" attribute? Enforced. Context menus on media elements? Dead. Picture-in-Picture on videos? Disabled — popping a video out of the reader defeated every other protection. Source URLs? Convertible to blob: URLs, hiding the original path. The currentSrc getter? Intercepted to return nothing useful.

Video Supplement — Behind the Scenes

Try Picture-in-Picture, downloading, or right-clicking with Media Protection enabled

"The needle's on the record," Aria said. "But you can't take the record home."

Chapter 18: Four Pipes

The Chrome extension was called "Tab Audio Recorder." Five stars. Two million installs. And it could record every sound that came out of a browser tab in perfect digital quality.

"It uses MediaRecorder," Aria said, her whiteboard now a nightmare of interconnected boxes and arrows. "But that's only one pipe. There are four."

She drew them:

Pipe one: new MediaRecorder(stream) — captures any MediaStream as a recording.
Pipe two: AudioContext.createMediaElementSource() — routes audio from a media element through the Web Audio API, where it can be captured.
Pipe three: element.captureStream() — creates a live MediaStream from any playing media or canvas element.
Pipe four: navigator.mediaDevices.getDisplayMedia() — the Screen Capture API. Every web-based screen recording tool uses this.

"Four pipes," Aria said. "Four ways to record protected content through JavaScript. We plug all four."

MediaRecorder was replaced with a proxy that threw a DOMException — the same error that real DRM-protected content produces. createMediaElementSource was blocked for elements inside the protected root. captureStream was intercepted on both media and canvas elements. And getDisplayMedia was blocked entirely.

"Native screen recording still works," Dion noted. "OBS, QuickTime."

"True," Aria said. "But we have watermarking for screenshots and nothing in JavaScript can stop a camera pointed at a screen. These four pipes are the ones we can plug. So we plug them."

Chapter 19: Tripwire

The attack that nearly broke them came from an unlikely source: a screenshot extension called GoFullPage. It didn't steal text. It didn't download files. It simply scrolled through the entire page and stitched together a full-page screenshot — and to coordinate its captures, it injected CSS onto the page's DOM elements.

Animation properties to time the scroll. Transition rules to smooth the capture. Transform styles to position elements for stitching. The extension was redecorating the living room to take better photos of the furniture.

"It's not stealing," Aria said slowly. "It's tampering. And we can detect tampering."

The tamper detection module was Aria's masterpiece. She placed invisible sentinel elements — tiny <div>s with zero dimensions, hidden from screen readers, distributed throughout the content like buried motion sensors. Each sentinel was watched by its own MutationObserver, looking for any unexpected change to its style or class attributes.

A second observer watched the content root for sentinel removal — because a clever extension might try to delete the sensors. And every two seconds, a backup check ran computed-style analysis on every sentinel, catching CSS injection that bypassed the mutation observers: injected stylesheets targeting elements by class or ID.

"Animation? Tripwire. Transform? Tripwire. Sentinel removed? Tripwire. Computed style anomaly? Tripwire." Aria was grinning. "The content blanks before the first frame is captured. GoFullPage gets a beautiful, high-resolution screenshot of a white rectangle."

They tested it against Nimbus, Awesome Screenshot, Fireshot. Every one triggered the sentinels. Every one captured blank pages.

"It's a museum with invisible laser grids," Dion said.

"Better," Aria corrected. "A museum where the paintings hide the moment someone reaches for a camera."

Epilogue: The Good Fence

Six months later. Board meeting. Aferdita's piracy dashboard was projected on the wall.

Automated scraping: down 94%. Casual copy-paste piracy: down 87%. Screenshot-based leaks: down 71% — and the ones that got through carried watermarks that led to account suspensions within hours. The forty-three-day preview reader was down to zero; the sand timer saw to that.

Dion stood at the front of the room. He looked tired — the good kind of tired, the kind that comes from building something worthwhile.

"We didn't build a fortress," he said. "A fortress keeps everyone out, and nobody wants that. We built a fence. Nineteen locks. Each one reasonable. Each one defeatable by someone determined enough. But together?" He paused. "Together, they make stealing harder than buying."

He clicked to the final slide:

Aria leaned over to Aferdita. "He rehearsed that list in the shower this morning."

"I know," Aferdita whispered back. "I could hear him through the wall."

The board voted unanimously to continue funding. Aferdita walked out into the autumn air and, for the first time in months, didn't check the piracy dashboard on her phone.

The fence was holding. The neighbors were behaving. And somewhere in Berlin, a graduate student was looking for a new thesis topic.
