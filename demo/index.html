Part I

The Thief in the Machine

The email arrived at 3:47 AM, as the worst ones always do.

Elena Vasquez, Chief Technology Officer of Meridian Publishing, stared at the subject line on her phone: "Your entire fall catalog is on a pirate site. All 240 titles." She sat up in bed, heart pounding, and opened the message.

It was true. Every single book Meridian had published in the last quarter — from the prize-winning literary fiction to the modest self-help guides — had been stripped of protection and uploaded in pristine EPUB format. The pirate site's download counter showed numbers that made her stomach turn.

She called Marcus first. Marcus Chen, her head of engineering, had warned her this would happen.

"The web reader," he said, not even sounding surprised. "I told the board. You put a book in a browser, and the browser is the user's machine. It's their DOM. Their JavaScript console. Their clipboard."

"So what do we do?" Elena asked. "Go back to dedicated apps? Our readers will revolt."

Marcus was quiet for a moment. "No," he said. "We get smarter."

* * *

Chapter 1: The Clipboard Wars

The first vulnerability Marcus investigated was the most obvious: copy and paste. A reader could simply select all the text on a page, press Ctrl+C, and paste an entire chapter into an email. The browser was delighted to assist.

"The clipboard is not your friend," Marcus told his team on Monday morning. "It's a highway out of your application, and right now there are no toll booths."

The solution was deceptively simple. Every copy event, every cut event, every keyboard shortcut that might invoke them — all intercepted. In block mode, the clipboard received nothing. In restrict mode, the reader could copy a sentence or two — enough to cite, enough to share a taste — but not a paragraph, never a page.

For publishers who wanted to be generous, there was the attribution feature: any copied text would arrive with a trailing line — "Copied from 'The Warden's Handbook' — © Meridian Publishing." The text carried its provenance with it, like a watermark on fine paper.

"You don't have to stop every thief. You just have to make stealing feel like stealing."

Chapter 2: The Phantom Page

The second attack vector was more insidious. A technically inclined reader could open the developer tools and simply read the DOM. Every word of the book was right there in the HTML.

"Imagine you're reading a physical book," Marcus explained to Elena. "You can see the page in front of you. But imagine that every page you're not looking at is written in cipher. The moment you turn to it, it decrypts. The moment you turn away, it scrambles again."

"That's insane," Elena said. "That's brilliant."

"That's text obfuscation," Marcus said.

The system tracked scroll position with surgical precision. Text within the visible viewport was presented in its original form. But text above or below — text that had scrolled out of sight — was shuffled into nonsense. The characters were all there, rearranged within each word, producing something that looked almost like language but was meaningless.

Anyone who dumped document.body.innerText would get a wall of gibberish sprinkled with a few legible sentences. The mutation observer — a sentinel watching for tampering — would re-scramble any text node that was manually restored.

Part II

The Invisible War

Chapter 3: Ghosts in the Machine

The third generation of attacks didn't come from humans at all. They came from scripts.

Marcus had been monitoring the server logs when he noticed the pattern: a single user account loading every page of every book, one after another, at precisely three-second intervals. No human reads that fast. No human reads twenty-six books in a single afternoon.

"Puppeteer," said Anika, the junior security engineer. "Or Playwright. They're using a headless browser to automate page turns and scrape the text."

She showed Marcus the detection cascade: WebDriver flags, headless indicators, automation-framework globals, missing browser plugins, screen dimension anomalies. Each check on its own might produce a false positive. Together, they formed a reliable signature.

"And we re-check every five seconds," Anika added, "because a clever attacker might inject the automation framework after page load."

Chapter 4: The Invisible Ink

Despite all their defenses, Marcus knew that some content would eventually escape. A determined reader with a camera pointed at their screen could capture any page.

"If we can't prevent all leaks," he told the team, "we need to be able to trace them."

The watermarking system operated on two levels. The first was visual: a near-transparent overlay containing the reader's user ID, repeated across the page. At 3% opacity, invisible during normal reading — but if someone posted a screenshot online, adjusting the levels in any image editor would reveal the identity of the leaker.

The second level was far more subtle. Zero-width Unicode characters — invisible characters that render as nothing on screen — were injected into the content at chosen intervals, encoding the reader's identity in binary. If someone copied text and pasted it anywhere, the zero-width characters traveled with it, invisible and indestructible.

"It's like invisible ink," Elena said when Marcus demonstrated it.

"It's better than invisible ink," Marcus replied. "Invisible ink can be washed away. These characters survive copy, paste, email forwarding, even OCR."

Chapter 5: The Voice in the Wire

The vulnerability report that arrived in April caught everyone off guard. A graduate student in Berlin had discovered that the Web Speech API could be used to extract book content. A script could feed each page into speechSynthesis.speak(), record the output, and run it through speech-to-text to reconstruct the book.

"That's three transformations," Anika said, impressed despite herself. "Text to speech, speech to recording, recording back to text. Who even thinks of that?"

"Graduate students with too much time," Marcus said. "Block it."

The speech synthesis module intercepted the browser's speak() method at the prototype level. In block mode, calls were silently swallowed. In restrict mode, utterances were truncated — enough for accessibility, not enough to extract a full page.

Chapter 6: The Ticking Clock

The final piece came from an unlikely source: marketing.

"We have a problem with our free preview program," said the VP of Marketing. "We give readers 24 hours. But 12% of them leave their tabs open indefinitely, reading the entire book without purchasing."

Marcus smiled. He already had the solution.

The content expiration module started a timer when a reading session began. As the end approached, a warning fired. When time expired, the content blanked — visibility hidden, the words vanishing like writing in sand. For paid readers, the session could be extended. For preview readers, extensions were limited.

Part III

Reasonable Boundaries

Epilogue

Six months after deploying the full suite, Elena pulled up the piracy monitoring dashboard. The numbers had changed dramatically. Not to zero — they would never reach zero. But the casual piracy, the opportunistic scraping, the automated extraction — all of it had dropped by over 80%.

"We didn't build a fortress," Marcus said during his presentation to the board. "We built a fence. A good fence. The kind that makes good neighbors."

"Sixteen modules. Zero required dependencies. Framework-agnostic. Tree-shakable. Drop it into any web reader in the world."

Elena watched the board members' faces and saw something she hadn't seen in a long time: confidence. Not the false confidence of believing a problem was solved, but the quiet confidence of knowing it was managed.

That was enough. That had always been enough.
